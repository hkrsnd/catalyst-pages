What do these metrics mean?
MW (Da)Molecular Weight (MW) — total mass of the molecule in Daltons. Drugs typically need MW < 500 Da for good oral absorption (Lipinski's Rule).
LogPLogP — a measure of how fat-soluble (lipophilic) a molecule is. Higher = more greasy. Ideally ≤ 5 for oral drugs. The optimization goal here is to minimize LogP.
HBDHydrogen Bond Donors (HBD) — groups like OH or NH that can donate hydrogen bonds. Fewer donors generally means better membrane permeability. Lipinski limit: ≤ 5.
HBAHydrogen Bond Acceptors (HBA) — atoms like O or N that can accept hydrogen bonds. Lipinski limit: ≤ 10.
tPSATopological Polar Surface Area (tPSA) — the surface area of polar atoms (N, O, S and attached H). Measured in Å². Low tPSA (< 140) favors oral absorption.
Rot. BondsRotatable Bonds — single bonds that allow free rotation, making the molecule flexible. Fewer (≤ 10) is better for oral bioavailability.
QEDQuantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness (QED) — a score from 0 to 1 combining multiple drug-likeness properties. Higher = more drug-like. Most approved drugs score > 0.3.
Lipinski Viol.Lipinski Violations — how many of Lipinski's 'Rule of Five' criteria the molecule fails (MW, LogP, HBD, HBA). Zero violations is ideal; ≤ 1 is acceptable.

4. Tactical suggestions for the chemistry team

  • Introduce polar substituents on the phenyl rings (e.g., –F, –Cl, –CF₃ are still lipophilic; better: –OH, –OMe, –NH₂, –CONH₂).
  • Replace one phenyl with a hetero‑aryl (pyridine, pyrimidine, indole) – adds H‑bond acceptors/donors and reduces logP while preserving aromatic character and similarity.
  • Shorten or cap the alkyl chain attached to the central carbonyl (e.g., replace the propyl “CCC” with “CC” or a methyl) – reduces hydrophobic surface.
  • Add a small polar side‑chain (e.g., –OCH₂CH₂OH) on the ether linker; this increases tPSA and HBA count with minimal impact on similarity.
  • Avoid large scaffold jumps (e.g., introducing bulky heterocycles or long polar linkers) that would push similarity below 0.4.

Summary & Recommendations

Both proposals are not outright disqualified, but each carries a distinct ADMET challenge that should be mitigated before committing to later‑stage development.
  • Proposal 1 – Focus on formulation strategies (e.g., solid‑dispersion, lipid‑based delivery) or modest polarity‑enhancing modifications (e.g., a small polar substituent) to bring logP below 5 and improve solubility.
  • Proposal 2 – Prioritise early metabolic and toxicity profiling:
- In‑vitro Phase II assays (glucuronidation, sulfation) to gauge clearance rate.
- Reactive metabolite screens (e.g., glutathione trapping) to assess quinone formation risk.
- If the phenol proves problematic, consider a bioisosteric replacement (e.g., a fluorine or a heteroaryl that mimics the H‑bonding without the redox liability).

Proceed with CAUTION for both, but allocate resources accordingly: solubility/absorption work for Proposal 1, and metabolic‑toxicity work for Proposal 2.

What do these metrics mean?
MW (Da)Molecular Weight (MW) — total mass of the molecule in Daltons. Drugs typically need MW < 500 Da for good oral absorption (Lipinski's Rule).
LogPLogP — a measure of how fat-soluble (lipophilic) a molecule is. Higher = more greasy. Ideally ≤ 5 for oral drugs. The optimization goal here is to minimize LogP.
HBDHydrogen Bond Donors (HBD) — groups like OH or NH that can donate hydrogen bonds. Fewer donors generally means better membrane permeability. Lipinski limit: ≤ 5.
HBAHydrogen Bond Acceptors (HBA) — atoms like O or N that can accept hydrogen bonds. Lipinski limit: ≤ 10.
tPSATopological Polar Surface Area (tPSA) — the surface area of polar atoms (N, O, S and attached H). Measured in Å². Low tPSA (< 140) favors oral absorption.
Rot. BondsRotatable Bonds — single bonds that allow free rotation, making the molecule flexible. Fewer (≤ 10) is better for oral bioavailability.
QEDQuantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness (QED) — a score from 0 to 1 combining multiple drug-likeness properties. Higher = more drug-like. Most approved drugs score > 0.3.
Lipinski Viol.Lipinski Violations — how many of Lipinski's 'Rule of Five' criteria the molecule fails (MW, LogP, HBD, HBA). Zero violations is ideal; ≤ 1 is acceptable.

What needs the most improvement?

  • logP: At 3.64 the molecule is moderately lipophilic. A reduction of ~0.5–1.0 units in this first round would give us a clear margin for a second‑round fine‑tune and would already move the compound toward the “low‑logP” region often desired for oral drugs.
  • Secondary considerations: Any logP‑lowering change must not catastrophically degrade the other metrics. For example, adding a highly polar group that drives logP down but pushes tPSA > 100 Å² or introduces multiple H‑bond donors would be counter‑productive.

Recommended design strategies for Round 1

  • Trim or replace hydrophobic fragments
- Shorten the propyl chain (CCC) to an ethyl or methyl (CC or C).
- Replace the phenyl ring with a less‑hydrophobic heteroaryl (e.g., pyridine, pyrimidine) while preserving the fused thienobenzene core to stay ≥ 0.4 similar.
  • Introduce modest polarity
- Add a single hydrogen‑bond donor/acceptor that is not too polar (e.g., a hydroxyl on the side‑chain or a carbonyl on the aromatic system).
- Consider converting the nitrile (C#N) to an amide (C(=O)N) – this adds one H‑bond acceptor and donor, modestly raises tPSA, and can drop logP by ~0.3–0.5.

- Any substitution should keep the fused thienobenzene core intact; changes limited to the aliphatic side‑chain or peripheral aromatic substitution will typically retain a Tanimoto similarity ≥ 0.4.

The Challenge

Why molecular optimization is hard

Drug discovery requires optimizing molecules across multiple competing properties — reducing lipophilicity while maintaining drug-likeness, lowering molecular weight while preserving biological activity. These multi-objective optimization problems are inherently difficult because improving one property often degrades another. Traditional single-metric approaches miss critical trade-offs, leading to molecules that look good on paper but fail in practice.

Multi-Objective Trade-offs

Reducing LogP (lipophilicity) often increases molecular weight. Improving QED (drug-likeness) may reduce synthetic accessibility. Balancing these requires expert judgment.

Chemical Validity

Proposed modifications must produce chemically valid, synthetically feasible molecules. Invalid suggestions waste optimization rounds and make no progress toward the goal.

ADMET Properties

A drug candidate must have favorable Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, Excretion, and Toxicity profiles. Ignoring ADMET leads to late-stage clinical failures.

How Catalyst Works

A multi-agent architecture for molecular optimization

Catalyst decomposes the optimization problem into specialized roles. Five AI agents collaborate in a structured loop — proposing modifications, critiquing them, evaluating properties, and deciding whether to continue or stop. Each agent brings domain expertise that no single model can match alone.

PL

Project Lead

Orchestrates the optimization process. Analyzes objectives, delegates tasks, and makes final selection decisions (ADVANCE or TERMINATE).

MC

Medicinal Chemist

Proposes structural modifications to the molecule based on chemical intuition and optimization objectives. The core creative agent.

CR

Critic

Reviews proposals for chemical validity, synthetic feasibility, and potential issues. Suggests improvements before evaluation.

CC

Computational Chemist

Evaluates proposed molecules by computing molecular properties (LogP, QED, MW, tPSA, HBD, HBA) and Tanimoto similarity.

AD

ADMET Specialist

Assesses pharmacokinetic properties — Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, Excretion, and Toxicity — to guide drug-like optimization.

How We Measure

All metrics are computed in silico — no wet-lab experiments required

Every molecular property reported by Catalyst is computed purely from the SMILES string (a text encoding of the molecular graph) using RDKit, an open-source cheminformatics library. Given only the atoms and bonds, RDKit can calculate physicochemical descriptors in milliseconds. Some metrics are deterministic calculations from molecular structure; others are predictive models trained on experimental data.

No synthesis, no assays, no instruments. The Computational Chemist agent receives pre-computed RDKit results before its turn. This means every candidate molecule is evaluated objectively and instantly — the agents discuss and reason about these ground-truth numbers, not estimates.

Metric RDKit Function Type How It Works
MW (Da) Descriptors.MolWt Exact Sum of atomic masses from the molecular formula — pure arithmetic on atom counts.
LogP Descriptors.MolLogP Predictive Wildman–Crippen method: assigns each atom a fragment-based hydrophobicity contribution and sums them. Trained on experimental partition coefficients.
HBD CalcNumHBD Exact Counts OH and NH groups via SMARTS pattern matching on the molecular graph.
HBA CalcNumHBA Exact Counts nitrogen and oxygen atoms that can accept hydrogen bonds — topological pattern matching.
tPSA (Å²) Descriptors.TPSA Predictive Ertl method: fragment-based sum of tabulated surface-area contributions for each polar atom (N, O, S, P and their hydrogens).
QED QED.qed Composite Combines MW, LogP, HBD, HBA, tPSA, rotatable bonds, aromatic rings, and structural alerts through a desirability function trained on ~700 approved drugs.
Lipinski Violations CheckLipinski Exact Checks four thresholds: MW > 500, LogP > 5, HBD > 5, HBA > 10. Count of violations (0 = ideal).
Similarity TanimotoSimilarity Exact Each molecule is hashed into a 2048-bit Morgan fingerprint (ECFP4, radius 2). Tanimoto coefficient = |intersection| / |union| of the two bit vectors.

Metric Glossary
LogP (Lipophilicity)
Partition coefficient measuring fat-solubility vs. water-solubility. Drug-like range: −0.4 to 5.6. Lower is generally better for absorption.
QED (Drug-Likeness)
Quantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness. Ranges from 0 to 1; higher values indicate more drug-like molecular profiles.
Molecular Weight (MW)
Total molecular mass in Daltons. Lipinski's Rule of Five suggests MW < 500 Da for oral bioavailability.
tPSA (Polar Surface Area)
Topological polar surface area in Angstroms squared. Affects membrane permeability; tPSA < 140 is preferred for oral drugs.
HBD / HBA
Hydrogen Bond Donors and Acceptors. Lipinski suggests HBD ≤ 5 and HBA ≤ 10 for good oral absorption.
Validity Rate
Fraction of proposed SMILES strings that parse into valid, chemically meaningful molecules. Higher validity means fewer wasted optimization rounds.
Tanimoto Similarity
Molecular fingerprint similarity between starting and optimized molecules. Ranges from 0 (completely different) to 1 (identical).
Success Rate
Fraction of optimization tasks where the final molecule met all specified optimization objectives.

What do these metrics mean?
MW (Da)Molecular Weight (MW) — total mass of the molecule in Daltons. Drugs typically need MW < 500 Da for good oral absorption (Lipinski's Rule).
LogPLogP — a measure of how fat-soluble (lipophilic) a molecule is. Higher = more greasy. Ideally ≤ 5 for oral drugs. The optimization goal here is to minimize LogP.
HBDHydrogen Bond Donors (HBD) — groups like OH or NH that can donate hydrogen bonds. Fewer donors generally means better membrane permeability. Lipinski limit: ≤ 5.
HBAHydrogen Bond Acceptors (HBA) — atoms like O or N that can accept hydrogen bonds. Lipinski limit: ≤ 10.
tPSATopological Polar Surface Area (tPSA) — the surface area of polar atoms (N, O, S and attached H). Measured in Å². Low tPSA (< 140) favors oral absorption.
Rot. BondsRotatable Bonds — single bonds that allow free rotation, making the molecule flexible. Fewer (≤ 10) is better for oral bioavailability.
QEDQuantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness (QED) — a score from 0 to 1 combining multiple drug-likeness properties. Higher = more drug-like. Most approved drugs score > 0.3.
Lipinski Viol.Lipinski Violations — how many of Lipinski's 'Rule of Five' criteria the molecule fails (MW, LogP, HBD, HBA). Zero violations is ideal; ≤ 1 is acceptable.

1. Primary Goal – Lower LogP

  • Target range: ≤ 4.0 (ideally 3.0–3.5) while staying ≥ 0.3 similarity.
  • Key levers:
- Replace or remove the CF₃ group (highly lipophilic). Consider a fluorine, chlorine, or a small polar substituent (e.g., –OH, –NH₂, –CONH₂).
- Trim peripheral aromatic rings that contribute most to hydrophobic surface (e.g., the phenyl attached to the amide). Substituting with a hetero‑aryl (pyridine, pyrimidine) or a smaller heterocycle can cut LogP.
- Introduce polar heteroatoms (e.g., –OH, –NH‑, –OCH₃) at positions that do not disrupt the core binding interactions. Each added H‑bond donor/acceptor typically drops LogP by ~0.3–0.5 units.

2. Secondary Goals – Control Molecular Weight & Improve Drug‑likeness

  • Weight management: Any substitution that removes heavy atoms (CF₃, phenyl) will also lower MW, moving us toward the 500 Da limit. Avoid adding large alkyl chains or bulky substituents.
  • Increase QED: By lowering LogP and MW, QED will naturally rise. Adding a modest polar group (e.g., –OH) can also improve QED without inflating MW.
  • Maintain H‑bond balance: Keep HBD ≤ 3 and HBA ≤ 10; the current counts (2/6) are fine, but any new polar groups should not push HBD > 3.

3. Tertiary Constraints – Similarity & Synthetic Feasibility

  • Similarity ≥ 0.3: Any design must retain the core bicyclic hetero‑aryl scaffold (the quinazoline‑like core and the central amide linkage). Modifications should be limited to peripheral substituents (CF₃ phenyl, the outer phenyl ring, or small side‑chain tweaks).
  • Synthetic tractability: Prefer changes that can be achieved by standard functional‑group interconversions (e.g., halogen‑to‑hydroxyl, CF₃‑to‑F, phenyl‑to‑pyridine).

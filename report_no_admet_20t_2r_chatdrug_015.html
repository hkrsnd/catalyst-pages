What do these metrics mean?
MW (Da)Molecular Weight (MW) — total mass of the molecule in Daltons. Drugs typically need MW < 500 Da for good oral absorption (Lipinski's Rule).
LogPLogP — a measure of how fat-soluble (lipophilic) a molecule is. Higher = more greasy. Ideally ≤ 5 for oral drugs. The optimization goal here is to minimize LogP.
HBDHydrogen Bond Donors (HBD) — groups like OH or NH that can donate hydrogen bonds. Fewer donors generally means better membrane permeability. Lipinski limit: ≤ 5.
HBAHydrogen Bond Acceptors (HBA) — atoms like O or N that can accept hydrogen bonds. Lipinski limit: ≤ 10.
tPSATopological Polar Surface Area (tPSA) — the surface area of polar atoms (N, O, S and attached H). Measured in Å². Low tPSA (< 140) favors oral absorption.
Rot. BondsRotatable Bonds — single bonds that allow free rotation, making the molecule flexible. Fewer (≤ 10) is better for oral bioavailability.
QEDQuantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness (QED) — a score from 0 to 1 combining multiple drug-likeness properties. Higher = more drug-like. Most approved drugs score > 0.3.
Lipinski Viol.Lipinski Violations — how many of Lipinski's 'Rule of Five' criteria the molecule fails (MW, LogP, HBD, HBA). Zero violations is ideal; ≤ 1 is acceptable.

1. Primary objective – LogP reduction

  • Why? LogP is the only metric we are explicitly trying to improve. A reduction of ≥ 0.5 units will move the molecule off the current Pareto front and give us a tangible gain.
  • How? Introduce polar/ionizable functionality or remove hydrophobic surface while staying within the similarity window.
- Add H‑bond donors/acceptors (e.g., hydroxyl, amide, sulfonamide, morpholine, pyridine nitrogen) on the peripheral phenyl rings or on the aliphatic bridge.
- Replace one or both fluorine atoms with more polar substituents (e.g., –OH, –NH₂, –OCH₃) or delete a phenyl ring altogether if similarity remains ≥ 0.4.
- Shorten the alkyl linker (C‑C‑C) that connects the central bicyclic core to the distal phenyl; fewer carbons = lower logP.
- Introduce hetero‑atoms into the aromatic rings (e.g., pyridine, pyrimidine) to increase polarity without dramatically changing shape.

2. Secondary constraints – Maintain drug‑like profile

  • Molecular weight should stay comfortably below 500 Da; any added polar group must not push the weight > 450 Da (to keep a safety margin).
  • H‑bond balance: keep donors ≤ 5 and acceptors ≤ 10. Adding one OH or one amide carbonyl is safe.
  • tPSA can be raised modestly (up to ~80 Å²) to aid logP reduction, but avoid > 140 Å² which would hurt permeability.
  • Rotatable bonds: avoid excessive flexibility; each new rotatable bond should be justified by a clear logP gain.
  • QED: aim to keep it ≥ 0.6; drastic structural changes that lower QED should be avoided.
  • Lipinski violations: must remain zero.

3. Similarity requirement – ≥ 0.4

  • Use a fingerprint‑based Tanimoto similarity check (e.g., Morgan radius 2, 2048 bits). Any proposed scaffold that removes an entire phenyl ring or replaces the core bicyclic system will likely fall below 0.4. Safer modifications are:
- Substituent swaps on existing rings.
- Small hetero‑atom insertions (e.g., converting a phenyl to a pyridine).
- Appending a modest polar side‑chain (e.g., –CH₂OH) at a position already bearing a carbon.

4. Concrete suggestions for the Computational Chemist

  • Hydroxylate one fluorophenyl: replace the –F on the distal phenyl (c3ccc(F)cc3) with –OH (or –OCH₃). Expected logP drop ≈ 0.4–0.6, similarity > 0.5.

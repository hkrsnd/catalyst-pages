What do these metrics mean?
MW (Da)Molecular Weight (MW) — total mass of the molecule in Daltons. Drugs typically need MW < 500 Da for good oral absorption (Lipinski's Rule).
LogPLogP — a measure of how fat-soluble (lipophilic) a molecule is. Higher = more greasy. Ideally ≤ 5 for oral drugs. The optimization goal here is to minimize LogP.
HBDHydrogen Bond Donors (HBD) — groups like OH or NH that can donate hydrogen bonds. Fewer donors generally means better membrane permeability. Lipinski limit: ≤ 5.
HBAHydrogen Bond Acceptors (HBA) — atoms like O or N that can accept hydrogen bonds. Lipinski limit: ≤ 10.
tPSATopological Polar Surface Area (tPSA) — the surface area of polar atoms (N, O, S and attached H). Measured in Å². Low tPSA (< 140) favors oral absorption.
Rot. BondsRotatable Bonds — single bonds that allow free rotation, making the molecule flexible. Fewer (≤ 10) is better for oral bioavailability.
QEDQuantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness (QED) — a score from 0 to 1 combining multiple drug-likeness properties. Higher = more drug-like. Most approved drugs score > 0.3.
Lipinski Viol.Lipinski Violations — how many of Lipinski's 'Rule of Five' criteria the molecule fails (MW, LogP, HBD, HBA). Zero violations is ideal; ≤ 1 is acceptable.

1. Primary objective – lower logP

  • Target: bring logP down to ≤ 4.0 (≈ 0.9 units lower) in this round. This is a realistic first‑step reduction while still preserving the core scaffold.
  • Why: A logP of ~4.9 is relatively high for oral drug‑likeness; dropping below 4 will improve solubility and reduce the risk of off‑target lipophilic binding.

3. Tactical ways to achieve a lower logP while respecting similarity ≥ 0.4

  • Introduce polar heteroatoms on the periphery of the scaffold (e.g., replace a phenyl hydrogen with –OH, –NH₂, or –OCH₃).
  • Swap the chlorine for a less lipophilic substituent (F, H, or a small polar group).
  • Add a carbonyl or sulfonyl to the aromatic system (e.g., convert a C–H to C=O or –SO₂Me) – this adds H‑bond acceptors and reduces hydrophobic surface.
  • Replace the ethoxy‑carbonyl (CCOC(=O)–) with a more polar amide or carbamate (e.g., –NHCOCH₃) – retains the same connectivity but raises polarity.
  • Introduce a small basic side‑chain (e.g., a morpholine or piperazine pendant) that can be attached via a short linker to the existing piperidine nitrogen; this adds H‑bond donors/acceptors and reduces logP, but must be checked for similarity (the core bicyclic‑aryl scaffold must remain intact).

4. What NOT to do in this round

  • Drastic scaffold changes (e.g., removing the fused aromatic system) – would likely drop similarity below 0.4.
  • Heavy addition of bulky lipophilic groups (e.g., long alkyl chains) – would increase logP and possibly violate MW limits.
  • Introduce many rotatable bonds – could lower QED and increase flexibility beyond the desired range.

5. Success criteria for Round 1

  • At least one proposed analogue with logP ≤ 4.0 and similarity ≥ 0.4 to the parent.
  • All other drug‑likeness metrics remain within the ranges above (no new Lipinski violations, QED ≥ 0.6).
  • The analogue should not be dominated by the current best (i.e., it must improve logP without worsening any other metric to a degree that makes it inferior overall).

  • Prioritise those that achieve the logP target while preserving similarity and overall drug‑likeness.

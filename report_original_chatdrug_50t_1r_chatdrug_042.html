What do these metrics mean?
MW (Da)Molecular Weight (MW) — total mass of the molecule in Daltons. Drugs typically need MW < 500 Da for good oral absorption (Lipinski's Rule).
LogPLogP — a measure of how fat-soluble (lipophilic) a molecule is. Higher = more greasy. Ideally ≤ 5 for oral drugs. The optimization goal here is to minimize LogP.
HBDHydrogen Bond Donors (HBD) — groups like OH or NH that can donate hydrogen bonds. Fewer donors generally means better membrane permeability. Lipinski limit: ≤ 5.
HBAHydrogen Bond Acceptors (HBA) — atoms like O or N that can accept hydrogen bonds. Lipinski limit: ≤ 10.
tPSATopological Polar Surface Area (tPSA) — the surface area of polar atoms (N, O, S and attached H). Measured in Å². Low tPSA (< 140) favors oral absorption.
Rot. BondsRotatable Bonds — single bonds that allow free rotation, making the molecule flexible. Fewer (≤ 10) is better for oral bioavailability.
QEDQuantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness (QED) — a score from 0 to 1 combining multiple drug-likeness properties. Higher = more drug-like. Most approved drugs score > 0.3.
Lipinski Viol.Lipinski Violations — how many of Lipinski's 'Rule of Five' criteria the molecule fails (MW, LogP, HBD, HBA). Zero violations is ideal; ≤ 1 is acceptable.

1. Primary Goal – Reduce logP

  • Target range: logP < 3.5 would be a meaningful improvement (≈ 15 % reduction in predicted lipophilicity). Anything lower is better, but we must stay within the similarity limit.
  • How to achieve it:
1. Introduce polar functionality on the phenyl rings without breaking the core amide/ester linkage. Examples:
- Replace one or both chlorine atoms with hydroxyl, methoxy, or fluoro (the latter is less polar but can modestly lower logP).
- Add a hydrogen‑bond donor/acceptor (e.g., –OH, –NH₂, –CONH₂) ortho or para to the existing substituents.
2. Reduce aromatic surface area slightly:
- Convert one phenyl ring to a pyridine or pyrimidine (adds a nitrogen, raises polarity, and still scores high similarity).
3. Swap the acetyl ester for a more polar carbonyl (e.g., replace the acetate with a carbamate or hydroxy‑acetate).
4. Add a small hetero‑atom‑rich side chain (e.g., –CH₂OH) at a position that does not disrupt the amide bond.

2. Secondary Constraints – Keep the molecule “drug‑like”

  • Similarity ≥ 0.4: Any modification that retains the central acetyl‑aryl‑C(=O)‑NH‑aryl scaffold will comfortably meet this threshold. Avoid wholesale scaffold hopping (e.g., replacing both phenyls with aliphatic rings).

  • Hydrogen‑bond donors/acceptors: Adding one OH or NH₂ will increase HBD/HBA modestly (still well within Lipinski limits).

What do these metrics mean?
MW (Da)Molecular Weight (MW) — total mass of the molecule in Daltons. Drugs typically need MW < 500 Da for good oral absorption (Lipinski's Rule).
LogPLogP — a measure of how fat-soluble (lipophilic) a molecule is. Higher = more greasy. Ideally ≤ 5 for oral drugs. The optimization goal here is to minimize LogP.
HBDHydrogen Bond Donors (HBD) — groups like OH or NH that can donate hydrogen bonds. Fewer donors generally means better membrane permeability. Lipinski limit: ≤ 5.
HBAHydrogen Bond Acceptors (HBA) — atoms like O or N that can accept hydrogen bonds. Lipinski limit: ≤ 10.
tPSATopological Polar Surface Area (tPSA) — the surface area of polar atoms (N, O, S and attached H). Measured in Å². Low tPSA (< 140) favors oral absorption.
Rot. BondsRotatable Bonds — single bonds that allow free rotation, making the molecule flexible. Fewer (≤ 10) is better for oral bioavailability.
QEDQuantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness (QED) — a score from 0 to 1 combining multiple drug-likeness properties. Higher = more drug-like. Most approved drugs score > 0.3.
Lipinski Viol.Lipinski Violations — how many of Lipinski's 'Rule of Five' criteria the molecule fails (MW, LogP, HBD, HBA). Zero violations is ideal; ≤ 1 is acceptable.

1. Primary Objective – Lower logP

  • Why: logP = 6.0 is the only Lipinski violation and the sole optimization target. Reducing it below 5 will immediately remove the violation and improve oral‑availability prospects.
  • How: Introduce polarity without dramatically increasing size or compromising similarity:
- Add hydrogen‑bond donors/acceptors (e.g., hydroxyl, carbonyl, sulfonyl, amide, or hetero‑aryl groups) on the periphery of the molecule.
- Replace one of the two phenyl rings with a hetero‑aryl (pyridine, pyrimidine, thiazole) to retain aromatic character while adding nitrogen/heteroatoms.
- Shorten or cap the tert‑butyl‑like alkyl chain (the “CCC” fragment) with a more polar substituent (e.g., –CH₂OH, –OCH₃, –CONH₂).

2. Secondary Objectives – Maintain / modestly improve drug‑likeness

  • Molecular weight: Keep ≤ 500 Da. Any added polar group should be small (≤ 30 Da) to avoid overshooting.
  • tPSA: Aim for 30–70 Å². Adding one or two heteroatoms (N/O) will raise PSA into a more “drug‑like” window without hurting permeability.
  • QED: A higher QED (≥ 0.5) will be a natural side‑effect of lowering logP and raising PSA; monitor it but do not sacrifice similarity for a marginal QED gain.
  • Rotatable bonds: The current count (8) is acceptable; avoid adding long flexible linkers that would push the count > 10.

3. Similarity Constraint (≥ 0.4)

  • Any proposed scaffold modifications must retain the core stilbene‑type backbone (the central C=C attached to two phenyl rings) and the para‑O‑CH₂CH₂N(Me)₂ side chain, because these features dominate the fingerprint similarity.
  • Substitutions should be local (e.g., para‑ or meta‑substitutions on one phenyl ring, or replacement of a phenyl with a hetero‑aryl) rather than wholesale scaffold changes.

4. Concrete Design Directions for the Computational Chemist

  • Phenyl → Pyridine substitution
- Replace one phenyl ring (preferably the one not directly attached to the O‑alkyl side chain) with a 3‑pyridyl or 4‑pyridyl ring. This adds a nitrogen (H‑bond acceptor) and reduces logP by ~0.5–0.8 units.

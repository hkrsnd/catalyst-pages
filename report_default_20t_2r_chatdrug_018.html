What do these metrics mean?
MW (Da)Molecular Weight (MW) — total mass of the molecule in Daltons. Drugs typically need MW < 500 Da for good oral absorption (Lipinski's Rule).
LogPLogP — a measure of how fat-soluble (lipophilic) a molecule is. Higher = more greasy. Ideally ≤ 5 for oral drugs. The optimization goal here is to minimize LogP.
HBDHydrogen Bond Donors (HBD) — groups like OH or NH that can donate hydrogen bonds. Fewer donors generally means better membrane permeability. Lipinski limit: ≤ 5.
HBAHydrogen Bond Acceptors (HBA) — atoms like O or N that can accept hydrogen bonds. Lipinski limit: ≤ 10.
tPSATopological Polar Surface Area (tPSA) — the surface area of polar atoms (N, O, S and attached H). Measured in Å². Low tPSA (< 140) favors oral absorption.
Rot. BondsRotatable Bonds — single bonds that allow free rotation, making the molecule flexible. Fewer (≤ 10) is better for oral bioavailability.
QEDQuantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness (QED) — a score from 0 to 1 combining multiple drug-likeness properties. Higher = more drug-like. Most approved drugs score > 0.3.
Lipinski Viol.Lipinski Violations — how many of Lipinski's 'Rule of Five' criteria the molecule fails (MW, LogP, HBD, HBA). Zero violations is ideal; ≤ 1 is acceptable.

1. Primary objective – logP reduction

  • Target: bring logP down by ≥ 0.5 units (ideally ≥ 1.0) in this round.
  • Rationale: A single‑digit logP is still acceptable for drug‑like molecules, but a reduction of 0.5–1.0 will move the compound noticeably toward the desired region without demanding drastic scaffold changes.

2. Secondary, supporting objectives

Because we have only two rounds, any improvement in logP must not come at the expense of creating new, larger problems. The following properties should be maintained or modestly improved:

3. Structural guidance – how to achieve the logP drop while respecting the similarity threshold (≥ 0.3 Tanimoto)

  • Trim or replace non‑essential hydrophobic fragments
- The phenyl‑fused heterocycle (c2cn(C)c3ccccc23) contributes heavily to lipophilicity. Consider substituting a phenyl ring with a pyridine or pyrimidine or removing a methyl on that heterocycle.
- Keep the core quinazolin‑like scaffold (the central fused hetero‑aromatic system) because it carries most of the similarity weight.
  • Introduce polar substituents on the aromatic rings
- Add a hydroxy (–OH) or methoxy (–OCH₃) on the outer phenyl ring (the one bearing the CO‑ group) if not already present, or replace the existing methoxy with a hydroxy to increase polarity without large steric changes.
- Consider replacing one N‑methyl on the tertiary amine side‑chain with a hydroxyethyl (e.g., –N(CH₃)CH₂CH₂OH) to add H‑bond donors/acceptors and lower logP.
  • Shorten the flexible tertiary‑amine side chain
- The N(C)CCN(C)C dimethyl‑propyl‑dimethylamine contributes 2 rotatable bonds and hydrophobic surface. Replace one N‑methyl with a hydrogen (i.e., convert to secondary amine) or truncate the chain by one carbon. This reduces rotatable bonds and logP while preserving the basic nitrogen needed for similarity.
  • Maintain the key pharmacophore
- The amide‑linked C=C conjugated system and the aryl‑NH‑quinazoline hinge are essential for activity and for similarity scoring. Do not alter these bonds.

4. Decision criteria for the computational chemist’s proposals

  • LogP must be ≤ 4.0 (≥ 0.4 reduction) and preferably ≤ 3.8.
  • Similarity to the starting SMILES must be ≥ 0.30 (Tanimoto).
  • MW should not increase; a reduction of ≥ 10 Da is a bonus.
  • Rotatable bonds ≤ 12 (no increase).
  • tPSA ≥ 90 Å² (increase acceptable).
  • QED > 0.25 (any improvement).
  • Lipinski violations: aim for 0; if still 1, it must still be the MW violation only.

  • Primary: Achieve a measurable logP drop (≥ 0.5) while staying ≥ 0.30 similar to the parent.
  • Secondary: Slightly lower MW, keep rotatable bonds ≤ 12, maintain or raise tPSA, and improve QED.
  • Tactics: Replace or de‑alkylate the most lipophilic aromatic fragment, add polar groups (OH, carbonyl, or hydroxy‑ethyl), and shorten the tertiary‑amine side chain.

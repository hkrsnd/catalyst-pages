What do these metrics mean?
MW (Da)Molecular Weight (MW) — total mass of the molecule in Daltons. Drugs typically need MW < 500 Da for good oral absorption (Lipinski's Rule).
LogPLogP — a measure of how fat-soluble (lipophilic) a molecule is. Higher = more greasy. Ideally ≤ 5 for oral drugs. The optimization goal here is to minimize LogP.
HBDHydrogen Bond Donors (HBD) — groups like OH or NH that can donate hydrogen bonds. Fewer donors generally means better membrane permeability. Lipinski limit: ≤ 5.
HBAHydrogen Bond Acceptors (HBA) — atoms like O or N that can accept hydrogen bonds. Lipinski limit: ≤ 10.
tPSATopological Polar Surface Area (tPSA) — the surface area of polar atoms (N, O, S and attached H). Measured in Å². Low tPSA (< 140) favors oral absorption.
Rot. BondsRotatable Bonds — single bonds that allow free rotation, making the molecule flexible. Fewer (≤ 10) is better for oral bioavailability.
QEDQuantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness (QED) — a score from 0 to 1 combining multiple drug-likeness properties. Higher = more drug-like. Most approved drugs score > 0.3.
Lipinski Viol.Lipinski Violations — how many of Lipinski's 'Rule of Five' criteria the molecule fails (MW, LogP, HBD, HBA). Zero violations is ideal; ≤ 1 is acceptable.

Concrete Design Strategies for This Round

  • Introduce a polar substituent on the cyclopropyl ring
Replace the cyclopropyl (C3CC3) with a hydroxymethyl‑cyclopropyl or a morpholine‑linked moiety. This adds an H‑bond donor/acceptor, raises tPSA, and should lower log P while keeping the core scaffold intact (similarity > 0.4).
  • Modify the ester side‑chain
Convert the methyl ester COC(=O) to a hydroxy‑ethyl ester or a carboxylic acid (if synthetic feasibility permits). The extra OH or carboxylate will increase polarity and reduce log P, but we must watch the overall charge at physiological pH.
  • Add a small hetero‑aryl substituent
Replace the thienyl‑containing heterocycle c2cnc(C3CC3)s2 with a pyridine or pyrimidine bearing a fluoro or hydroxyl substituent. Fluorine can modestly affect log P, while a hydroxyl will directly increase polarity. The hetero‑aryl core remains similar enough to preserve the similarity score.
  • Introduce a single hydroxyl on the piperidine‑like ring
The existing chiral carbon already bears a hydroxyl; adding a second OH on the adjacent carbon (e.g., converting the CH to CH‑OH) could further lower log P. Care must be taken not to exceed the H‑bond donor limit (max 1 – 2 donors is acceptable).
  • Maintain the chiral centres
Preserve the stereochemistry of the two chiral centres; altering them would drastically lower similarity and is unnecessary for log P reduction.

What NOT to do

 Remove the existing carbonyl/ester – this would increase log P and likely reduce similarity. Introduce large lipophilic groups (e.g., long alkyl chains, bulky aromatic rings) – opposite of the log P goal. Add multiple basic amines – could raise log P (through salt formation) and increase the risk of additional Lipinski violations. Drastically change the heterocycle (e.g., replace the thienyl with a phenyl) – similarity would drop below the 0.4 threshold.

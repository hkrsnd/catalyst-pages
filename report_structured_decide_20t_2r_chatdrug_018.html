What do these metrics mean?
MW (Da)Molecular Weight (MW) — total mass of the molecule in Daltons. Drugs typically need MW < 500 Da for good oral absorption (Lipinski's Rule).
LogPLogP — a measure of how fat-soluble (lipophilic) a molecule is. Higher = more greasy. Ideally ≤ 5 for oral drugs. The optimization goal here is to minimize LogP.
HBDHydrogen Bond Donors (HBD) — groups like OH or NH that can donate hydrogen bonds. Fewer donors generally means better membrane permeability. Lipinski limit: ≤ 5.
HBAHydrogen Bond Acceptors (HBA) — atoms like O or N that can accept hydrogen bonds. Lipinski limit: ≤ 10.
tPSATopological Polar Surface Area (tPSA) — the surface area of polar atoms (N, O, S and attached H). Measured in Å². Low tPSA (< 140) favors oral absorption.
Rot. BondsRotatable Bonds — single bonds that allow free rotation, making the molecule flexible. Fewer (≤ 10) is better for oral bioavailability.
QEDQuantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness (QED) — a score from 0 to 1 combining multiple drug-likeness properties. Higher = more drug-like. Most approved drugs score > 0.3.
Lipinski Viol.Lipinski Violations — how many of Lipinski's 'Rule of Five' criteria the molecule fails (MW, LogP, HBD, HBA). Zero violations is ideal; ≤ 1 is acceptable.

3. Structural guidance – how to achieve the logP drop while respecting the similarity threshold (≥ 0.3)

  • Trim or replace highly lipophilic groups
- Methoxy (‑OCH₃) on the phenyl ring contributes to logP; consider demethylation to a phenol (‑OH) or removal altogether.
- N,N‑dimethyl‑propyl‑amine side chain (N(C)CCN(C)C) is a bulky, lipophilic tertiary amine. Replacing one methyl with a hydrogen (secondary amine) or shortening the alkyl linker (e.g., N(C)C or NHCH₃) will cut logP without drastically altering the scaffold.
  • Introduce polar heteroatoms
- Substituting a carbon of the fused bicyclic hetero‑aryl system with nitrogen (e.g., converting a phenyl to a pyridine) adds polarity and reduces logP while preserving aromatic character, keeping similarity above 0.3.
- Adding a small polar substituent (e.g., –OH, –NH₂, –CF₃→–F) on the peripheral phenyl can also help.
  • Reduce overall aromatic surface
- If feasible, replace the terminal phenyl‑fused heterocycle (c2cn(C)c3ccccc23) with a smaller hetero‑aryl (e.g., a quinazoline or pyrimidine) to cut both MW and logP. The core scaffold (the central phenyl linked to the amide and the heterocycle) must be retained to stay above the similarity threshold.
  • Maintain key pharmacophoric elements
- The amide‑conjugated alkene (NC(=O)/C=C/CN(C)C) and the central aniline‑linked heterocycle appear essential for activity. Do not modify these unless a minimal change (e.g., replace the terminal N‑methyl with hydrogen) can lower logP.

4. Decision criteria for the final round

When the computational chemist returns a set of candidate SMILES, evaluate each against the following checklist:
  • logP: Must be ≤ 3.0 (or at least 0.3 units lower than 3.53).
  • MW: ≤ 550 Da; if still > 500 Da, ensure no new violation is introduced.
  • Lipinski violations: ≤ 1 total.
  • Similarity: Tanimoto ≥ 0.30 to the starting molecule (not just the current best).
  • Pareto check: The candidate must not be dominated by any previously evaluated molecule on both logP and MW (or other hard constraints).
  • Overall balance: HBD/HBA, tPSA, RB remain within the ranges noted above.
Only a molecule that satisfies all hard constraints and delivers a clear logP improvement will be ADVANCE‑d. If none meet these criteria, we must TERMINATE the campaign, citing lack of viable logP reduction within the similarity and physicochemical limits.

  • Avoid adding heavy atoms or extra aromatic rings.
  • Keep the core scaffold recognizable (≥ 0.30 similarity).

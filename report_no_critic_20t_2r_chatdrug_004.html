What do these metrics mean?
MW (Da)Molecular Weight (MW) — total mass of the molecule in Daltons. Drugs typically need MW < 500 Da for good oral absorption (Lipinski's Rule).
LogPLogP — a measure of how fat-soluble (lipophilic) a molecule is. Higher = more greasy. Ideally ≤ 5 for oral drugs. The optimization goal here is to minimize LogP.
HBDHydrogen Bond Donors (HBD) — groups like OH or NH that can donate hydrogen bonds. Fewer donors generally means better membrane permeability. Lipinski limit: ≤ 5.
HBAHydrogen Bond Acceptors (HBA) — atoms like O or N that can accept hydrogen bonds. Lipinski limit: ≤ 10.
tPSATopological Polar Surface Area (tPSA) — the surface area of polar atoms (N, O, S and attached H). Measured in Å². Low tPSA (< 140) favors oral absorption.
Rot. BondsRotatable Bonds — single bonds that allow free rotation, making the molecule flexible. Fewer (≤ 10) is better for oral bioavailability.
QEDQuantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness (QED) — a score from 0 to 1 combining multiple drug-likeness properties. Higher = more drug-like. Most approved drugs score > 0.3.
Lipinski Viol.Lipinski Violations — how many of Lipinski's 'Rule of Five' criteria the molecule fails (MW, LogP, HBD, HBA). Zero violations is ideal; ≤ 1 is acceptable.

1. LogP Reduction – the only explicit objective

  • What drives the high logP?
- Two halogen‑substituted phenyl rings (Cl and F) and a fluorophenyl ether contribute substantial lipophilicity.
- The extended aromatic system (three fused rings plus two pendant phenyls) adds hydrophobic surface.
  • Strategic modifications to lower logP without breaking similarity (>0.30):
1. Replace the para‑F phenyl (OC‑c5cccc(F)c5) with a less lipophilic heteroaryl (e.g., pyridine, pyrimidine, or a fluorine‑free phenyl). This retains the ether linkage and aromatic character but reduces the fluorine‑driven logP boost.
2. Swap the chlorine on the central phenyl (c(Cl)c4) for a hydrogen or a modestly polar substituent (e.g., –OH, –OMe). This directly cuts ~0.5 logP units per halogen.
3. Introduce a polar “cap” on the sulfonyl‑methyl side chain – e.g., convert the terminal methyl of the sulfone (CS(=O)(=O)C…) to a hydroxymethyl or morpholine moiety. The sulfone already provides polarity; a small polar appendage can further lower logP while preserving the sulfonyl anchor.
4. Consider truncating one peripheral phenyl (e.g., remove the outermost phenyl of the –c2ccc3…c2 fragment) if similarity remains ≥ 0.30. This reduces aromatic surface area dramatically.
  • Quantitative expectation: Each halogen removal typically reduces logP by ~0.4–0.6 units; swapping a phenyl for a heteroaryl can shave another ~0.3–0.5 units. Combined, we should comfortably achieve a 0.8–1.2 logP reduction.

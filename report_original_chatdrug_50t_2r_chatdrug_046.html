What do these metrics mean?
MW (Da)Molecular Weight (MW) — total mass of the molecule in Daltons. Drugs typically need MW < 500 Da for good oral absorption (Lipinski's Rule).
LogPLogP — a measure of how fat-soluble (lipophilic) a molecule is. Higher = more greasy. Ideally ≤ 5 for oral drugs. The optimization goal here is to minimize LogP.
HBDHydrogen Bond Donors (HBD) — groups like OH or NH that can donate hydrogen bonds. Fewer donors generally means better membrane permeability. Lipinski limit: ≤ 5.
HBAHydrogen Bond Acceptors (HBA) — atoms like O or N that can accept hydrogen bonds. Lipinski limit: ≤ 10.
tPSATopological Polar Surface Area (tPSA) — the surface area of polar atoms (N, O, S and attached H). Measured in Å². Low tPSA (< 140) favors oral absorption.
Rot. BondsRotatable Bonds — single bonds that allow free rotation, making the molecule flexible. Fewer (≤ 10) is better for oral bioavailability.
QEDQuantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness (QED) — a score from 0 to 1 combining multiple drug-likeness properties. Higher = more drug-like. Most approved drugs score > 0.3.
Lipinski Viol.Lipinski Violations — how many of Lipinski's 'Rule of Five' criteria the molecule fails (MW, LogP, HBD, HBA). Zero violations is ideal; ≤ 1 is acceptable.

What the team should focus on this round

  • Introduce polar functionality on the periphery of the scaffold without breaking the core:
- Replace the terminal primary alcohol (CH₂OH) with a more polar group (e.g., hydroxymethyl → hydroxyethyl, hydroxy → carboxylic acid, primary amide, or morpholine).
- Add a hydrogen‑bond donor/acceptor on the aromatic ring (e.g., a para‑fluoro → para‑hydroxy or para‑methoxy → para‑hydroxy substitution).
  • Reduce hydrophobic surface:
- Slightly decrease aromatic bulk (e.g., replace one phenyl ring with a pyridine or a tetrahydrofuran ring) while preserving overall shape to stay above the similarity cut‑off.
- Consider introducing a heteroatom (N or O) into the fused bicyclic aromatic system (e.g., convert the naphthalene to a quinoline).
  • Maintain scaffold integrity:
- Keep the urea linkage and the piperidine ring unchanged; these are key for similarity and for preserving the high QED.
- Any modification should be a substituent change rather than a scaffold‑breaking transformation.
  • Balance polarity vs. size:
- Each added heteroatom should be weighed against the potential increase in MW and rotatable bonds.
- Prefer small polar groups (OH, NH₂, CONH₂, OCH₂CH₂) over bulky polar moieties (e.g., sulfonamides, long PEG chains) that would raise MW and rotatable bonds.
  • Generate a small, focused set of candidates (3‑5 analogues) that explore:
- Alcohol → carboxylic acid (adds one H‑bond donor/acceptor, raises TPSA, lowers logP).
- Aromatic OH substitution (adds polarity directly on the hydrophobic core).
- Aromatic heteroatom insertion (e.g., naphthalene → quinoline) to reduce logP while keeping aromatic surface.

What the team should do in this final round

  • Design/identify analogues that introduce additional polarity without large steric bulk
- Replace the phenyl ring (or the pyridine ring) with a more polar heteroaryl (e.g., pyrimidine, pyrazine, or a 1,2,4‑triazine) that still shares ≥ 0.4 Tanimoto similarity to the original aniline scaffold.
- Consider ortho‑ or meta‑substituted electron‑withdrawing groups (e.g., fluorine, chlorine, CF₃) that can modestly lower Log P while preserving similarity; however, be careful that halogens do not increase Log P.
  • Add a small polar substituent (e.g., a hydroxyl or methoxy) on the aromatic ring only if the resulting Tanimoto similarity stays above 0.4. A hydroxyl can drop Log P by ~0.3‑0.5 units with minimal impact on MW.
  • Avoid large aliphatic extensions (e.g., long alkyl chains) because they will raise Log P and MW and add rotatable bonds.
  • Run a quick similarity check on every proposed SMILES against the starting molecule; discard any < 0.4 before sending to the Computational Chemist.
  • Prioritise candidates that show a predicted Log P ≤ 0.8 while keeping all other metrics within the ranges above. If a candidate meets these criteria and is not dominated by the current best (i.e., it has lower Log P and equal or better values for the other properties), it should be put forward for evaluation.

Bottom‑line decision rule for this round

  • Advance a molecule only if:
- Predicted Log P ≤ 0.8 (or at least a ≥ 0.2‑unit improvement over 1.0), and
- Similarity ≥ 0.4, and
- No new Lipinski violations, and
- QED ≥ 0.80, and
- The molecule is not Pareto‑dominated by the current best (i.e., it improves Log P without worsening any other metric beyond acceptable tolerances).
  • Terminate the optimisation if none of the submitted candidates meet the above thresholds; given we are in the final (2/2) round, a lack of improvement would mean ending the campaign with the current best (log P = 1.0).

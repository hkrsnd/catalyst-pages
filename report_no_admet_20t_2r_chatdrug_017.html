What do these metrics mean?
MW (Da)Molecular Weight (MW) — total mass of the molecule in Daltons. Drugs typically need MW < 500 Da for good oral absorption (Lipinski's Rule).
LogPLogP — a measure of how fat-soluble (lipophilic) a molecule is. Higher = more greasy. Ideally ≤ 5 for oral drugs. The optimization goal here is to minimize LogP.
HBDHydrogen Bond Donors (HBD) — groups like OH or NH that can donate hydrogen bonds. Fewer donors generally means better membrane permeability. Lipinski limit: ≤ 5.
HBAHydrogen Bond Acceptors (HBA) — atoms like O or N that can accept hydrogen bonds. Lipinski limit: ≤ 10.
tPSATopological Polar Surface Area (tPSA) — the surface area of polar atoms (N, O, S and attached H). Measured in Å². Low tPSA (< 140) favors oral absorption.
Rot. BondsRotatable Bonds — single bonds that allow free rotation, making the molecule flexible. Fewer (≤ 10) is better for oral bioavailability.
QEDQuantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness (QED) — a score from 0 to 1 combining multiple drug-likeness properties. Higher = more drug-like. Most approved drugs score > 0.3.
Lipinski Viol.Lipinski Violations — how many of Lipinski's 'Rule of Five' criteria the molecule fails (MW, LogP, HBD, HBA). Zero violations is ideal; ≤ 1 is acceptable.

The new designs must retain the core bicyclic/heteroaryl scaffold (the anilide‑linked quinazoline‑like core and the dimethyl‑piperazine side chain). Modifications should be local (e.g., substituting the para‑methyl on the phenyl ring, replacing a hydrogen on the heteroaryl with a polar substituent, or tweaking the piperazine N‑alkyl groups). Avoid wholesale scaffold hopping or large ring‑system changes that would drop similarity below 0.4.

4. Concrete design directions for the Chemist

  • Replace the para‑methyl (‑CH₃) on the outer phenyl ring with a more polar group (e.g., ‑OH, ‑NH₂, ‑F, or a small heteroaryl). This directly reduces logP with minimal MW impact.
  • Introduce a heteroatom into the quinazoline‑like heteroaryl (e.g., swap a carbon for nitrogen or add a carbonyl) to raise tPSA and lower logP while preserving aromaticity.
  • Modify the N‑methyl on the piperazine to a less lipophilic substituent (e.g., replace with hydrogen or a small polar group like ‑OH or ‑CH₂OH). This can cut logP without breaking the scaffold.
  • Add a small polar substituent on the distal pyridine ring (‑Cl → ‑OH or ‑NH₂) to increase polarity without large MW penalty.
  • Consider de‑alkylating the amide nitrogen (replace the N‑H with a hydrogen or a small polar group) if it does not disrupt key H‑bonding.

What do these metrics mean?
MW (Da)Molecular Weight (MW) — total mass of the molecule in Daltons. Drugs typically need MW < 500 Da for good oral absorption (Lipinski's Rule).
LogPLogP — a measure of how fat-soluble (lipophilic) a molecule is. Higher = more greasy. Ideally ≤ 5 for oral drugs. The optimization goal here is to minimize LogP.
HBDHydrogen Bond Donors (HBD) — groups like OH or NH that can donate hydrogen bonds. Fewer donors generally means better membrane permeability. Lipinski limit: ≤ 5.
HBAHydrogen Bond Acceptors (HBA) — atoms like O or N that can accept hydrogen bonds. Lipinski limit: ≤ 10.
tPSATopological Polar Surface Area (tPSA) — the surface area of polar atoms (N, O, S and attached H). Measured in Å². Low tPSA (< 140) favors oral absorption.
Rot. BondsRotatable Bonds — single bonds that allow free rotation, making the molecule flexible. Fewer (≤ 10) is better for oral bioavailability.
QEDQuantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness (QED) — a score from 0 to 1 combining multiple drug-likeness properties. Higher = more drug-like. Most approved drugs score > 0.3.
Lipinski Viol.Lipinski Violations — how many of Lipinski's 'Rule of Five' criteria the molecule fails (MW, LogP, HBD, HBA). Zero violations is ideal; ≤ 1 is acceptable.

The project has 2 rounds total; we are at the start of round 1. The only explicit optimisation goal is lowering logP, but any change that also brings the molecule back into Lipinski compliance (MW ≤ 500, logP ≤ 5) will be highly valuable.

1. Primary objective – Reduce logP

  • Target range: ≤ 4.0 (a 1‑unit drop would already bring the compound into the “drug‑like” window and eliminate one Lipinski violation).
  • Key contributors to high logP in the current scaffold:
1. tert‑butyl group (C(CH₃)₃) – a large, highly hydrophobic alkyl.
2. Two fluorinated phenyl rings (each bearing two F atoms) – increase lipophilicity and molecular weight.
3. Sulfonyl‑aryl moiety (SO₂‑Ar) – adds both weight and hydrophobic surface.
  • Strategic moves (each should be explored in separate proposals):
- Replace the tert‑butyl with a smaller alkyl (e.g., isopropyl, methyl) or a polar substituent (e.g., hydroxymethyl).
- Remove one fluorine atom or replace the difluoro‑phenyl with a mono‑fluoro or unsubstituted phenyl.
- Swap the sulfonyl‑phenyl for a less bulky, more polar group (e.g., sulfonamide with a small alkyl, or a carbonyl‑linked heterocycle).

2. Secondary objectives – Bring the molecule back into Lipinski compliance

  • Molecular weight: Aim for ≤ 500 Da. The above modifications (especially removing a fluorine and/or the bulky sulfonyl‑phenyl) can shave ~30–50 Da each.
  • Maintain/Improve QED: While QED is not a hard objective, any reduction in size and polarity will typically raise the score.
  • Preserve essential pharmacophore: The hetero‑aryl core (the thiazine‑like ring fused to the pyrimidine) and the NH‑linked sulfonyl (or its replacement) appear crucial for activity. Modifications should keep the core intact and retain at least one H‑bond donor/acceptor pattern.

3. Similarity constraint – Stay ≥ 0.3 Tanimoto to the starting scaffold

  • The core hetero‑aryl system must be unchanged.
  • Substituent changes (tert‑butyl → methyl, removal of a fluorine, swapping the sulfonyl‑aryl) typically retain > 0.4 similarity, comfortably above the 0.3 cutoff.
  • Avoid wholesale scaffold hopping (e.g., replacing the entire phenyl‑sulfonyl fragment with a heterocycle that eliminates the core). Those would be rejected for being too dissimilar.

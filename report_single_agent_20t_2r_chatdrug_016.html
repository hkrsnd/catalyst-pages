What do these metrics mean?
MW (Da)Molecular Weight (MW) — total mass of the molecule in Daltons. Drugs typically need MW < 500 Da for good oral absorption (Lipinski's Rule).
LogPLogP — a measure of how fat-soluble (lipophilic) a molecule is. Higher = more greasy. Ideally ≤ 5 for oral drugs. The optimization goal here is to minimize LogP.
HBDHydrogen Bond Donors (HBD) — groups like OH or NH that can donate hydrogen bonds. Fewer donors generally means better membrane permeability. Lipinski limit: ≤ 5.
HBAHydrogen Bond Acceptors (HBA) — atoms like O or N that can accept hydrogen bonds. Lipinski limit: ≤ 10.
tPSATopological Polar Surface Area (tPSA) — the surface area of polar atoms (N, O, S and attached H). Measured in Å². Low tPSA (< 140) favors oral absorption.
Rot. BondsRotatable Bonds — single bonds that allow free rotation, making the molecule flexible. Fewer (≤ 10) is better for oral bioavailability.
QEDQuantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness (QED) — a score from 0 to 1 combining multiple drug-likeness properties. Higher = more drug-like. Most approved drugs score > 0.3.
Lipinski Viol.Lipinski Violations — how many of Lipinski's 'Rule of Five' criteria the molecule fails (MW, LogP, HBD, HBA). Zero violations is ideal; ≤ 1 is acceptable.

1. Primary objective – LogP reduction

Why now? LogP is the only explicit optimization goal. A meaningful improvement (≥ 0.5‑0.7 units) in a single round will put us on a viable Pareto front for the second round. How to achieve it?
Trim or polar‑functionalize the long C‑chain (the n‑pentyl attached to the heterocycle). Replacing one or two CH₂ units with an ether, hydroxyl, or amide will cut logP without destroying the core.
Introduce heteroatoms into the peripheral phenyl‑phenyl‑triazole region (e.g., a fluorine → methoxy swap, or a phenyl → pyridine). This adds polarity while preserving aromatic character and similarity.
Consider modest cyclisation or ring‑contraction that reduces exposed hydrophobic surface (e.g., converting the CCCCC side‑chain into a cyclopentyl or a morpholine ring).

2. Secondary constraints – Maintain drug‑likeness

 Molecular weight must stay ≤ 500 Da. Any added heteroatoms should not be accompanied by large substituents that push the MW > 500. Hydrogen‑bond donors/acceptors: keep HBD ≤ 5 and HBA ≤ 10. Adding a single OH or NH is acceptable; avoid multiple carbonyls or extra amines that would inflate HBA. TPSA: aim for 80‑120 Å². Small polar groups (OH, OMe, N‑oxide) will raise TPSA modestly, helping logP while staying in the sweet spot for permeability. Rotatable bonds: try not to increase beyond ~10; if we replace a flexible alkyl chain with a ring, rotatable count will actually drop, which is beneficial. QED: any change that improves polarity without excessive size or complexity should keep QED ≥ 0.55; ideally we see a modest bump (≥ 0.60).

3. Similarity constraint (≥ 0.4)

The core bicyclic urea‑pyrimidine scaffold and the central phenyl‑triazole motif must remain recognizable. Substitutions should be local (e.g., on the terminal alkyl chain or peripheral phenyl rings) rather than wholesale scaffold redesign. Use Tanimoto similarity calculations (provided by the Computational Chemist) to verify each proposal meets the 0.4 cutoff before submission.

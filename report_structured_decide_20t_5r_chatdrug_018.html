What do these metrics mean?
MW (Da)Molecular Weight (MW) — total mass of the molecule in Daltons. Drugs typically need MW < 500 Da for good oral absorption (Lipinski's Rule).
LogPLogP — a measure of how fat-soluble (lipophilic) a molecule is. Higher = more greasy. Ideally ≤ 5 for oral drugs. The optimization goal here is to minimize LogP.
HBDHydrogen Bond Donors (HBD) — groups like OH or NH that can donate hydrogen bonds. Fewer donors generally means better membrane permeability. Lipinski limit: ≤ 5.
HBAHydrogen Bond Acceptors (HBA) — atoms like O or N that can accept hydrogen bonds. Lipinski limit: ≤ 10.
tPSATopological Polar Surface Area (tPSA) — the surface area of polar atoms (N, O, S and attached H). Measured in Å². Low tPSA (< 140) favors oral absorption.
Rot. BondsRotatable Bonds — single bonds that allow free rotation, making the molecule flexible. Fewer (≤ 10) is better for oral bioavailability.
QEDQuantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness (QED) — a score from 0 to 1 combining multiple drug-likeness properties. Higher = more drug-like. Most approved drugs score > 0.3.
Lipinski Viol.Lipinski Violations — how many of Lipinski's 'Rule of Five' criteria the molecule fails (MW, LogP, HBD, HBA). Zero violations is ideal; ≤ 1 is acceptable.

1. Proposal 1 – Phenolic OH in place of the para‑methoxy

Absorption / Distribution

MW 542 Da – still above the 500 Da Lipinski cut‑off; oral permeability may be modest, but the drop in log P (4.14) and increase in PSA (≈102 Å²) move the molecule toward the “balanced” region of the log P‑PSA plot. H‑bond donors ↑ to 3 – extra donor can further reduce passive permeability, especially when combined with the phenolic OH (pKa ≈ 9–10, partially ionised at physiological pH).

Metabolism

Phenol is a classic metabolic liability: rapid Phase II conjugation (glucuronidation, sulfation) and possible oxidation to quinone‑type electrophiles. This can lead to high hepatic clearance and, in some cases, covalent protein binding. No obvious soft spots (e.g., anilines, nitro, Michael acceptors) beyond the phenol, so the rest of the scaffold remains comparable to the parent.

Toxicity

Phenolic quinone formation is a potential toxicity alert (reactive metabolite, oxidative stress). The risk can be mitigated by steric hindrance around the phenol; in this design the OH is para‑to a fairly bulky aniline‑linked heterocycle, which may provide some protection, but the risk is still present.

Excretion

 Higher polarity and lower log P favour renal elimination of conjugates, which is generally acceptable.

Overall ADMET impression

Strengths: Lower log P, modest MW reduction, no new hetero‑atom‑rich “PAINS” motifs. Weaknesses: Phenolic OH introduces a clear metabolic and potential toxicity liability; the extra H‑bond donor may impair oral absorption.
Verdict: CAUTION – the design is chemically sound and improves lipophilicity, but the phenol flag warrants further metabolic profiling (e.g., glucuronidation rate, quinone formation) before committing to in‑vivo studies.

What do these metrics mean?
MW (Da)Molecular Weight (MW) — total mass of the molecule in Daltons. Drugs typically need MW < 500 Da for good oral absorption (Lipinski's Rule).
LogPLogP — a measure of how fat-soluble (lipophilic) a molecule is. Higher = more greasy. Ideally ≤ 5 for oral drugs. The optimization goal here is to minimize LogP.
HBDHydrogen Bond Donors (HBD) — groups like OH or NH that can donate hydrogen bonds. Fewer donors generally means better membrane permeability. Lipinski limit: ≤ 5.
HBAHydrogen Bond Acceptors (HBA) — atoms like O or N that can accept hydrogen bonds. Lipinski limit: ≤ 10.
tPSATopological Polar Surface Area (tPSA) — the surface area of polar atoms (N, O, S and attached H). Measured in Å². Low tPSA (< 140) favors oral absorption.
Rot. BondsRotatable Bonds — single bonds that allow free rotation, making the molecule flexible. Fewer (≤ 10) is better for oral bioavailability.
QEDQuantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness (QED) — a score from 0 to 1 combining multiple drug-likeness properties. Higher = more drug-like. Most approved drugs score > 0.3.
Lipinski Viol.Lipinski Violations — how many of Lipinski's 'Rule of Five' criteria the molecule fails (MW, LogP, HBD, HBA). Zero violations is ideal; ≤ 1 is acceptable.

1. Primary objective – Reduce logP

  • Why: LogP = 2.0 is already modest, but the optimization goal is minimization. Even a 0.2–0.3 unit drop will move us toward a more polar, potentially better‑soluble candidate.
  • How: Introduce or expose polar functionality without dramatically increasing molecular weight or compromising the core scaffold:
- Add H‑bond donors/acceptors (e.g., hydroxyl, amide, morpholine, sulfonamide) on the peripheral phenyl ring or on the ether linker.
- Replace one or both fluorines (strongly lipophilic) with less lipophilic substituents (e.g., –Cl, –CH₃, –OH, –NH₂, hetero‑aryl). Fluorine removal can reduce logP while keeping aromatic character for similarity.
- Introduce hetero‑atoms into the aromatic system (e.g., pyridine, pyrimidine) to increase polarity and reduce cLogP.
- Shorten or replace the phenyl‑ether side chain with a more polar moiety (e.g., –OCH₂CH₂NH₂, –OCH₂CH₂OH) while preserving the overall scaffold.

2. Secondary constraints – Maintain similarity ≥ 0.4

  • Why: The similarity threshold is a hard filter; any proposal that drifts too far from the original scaffold will be rejected automatically.
  • How: Keep the core heterocycle (isoxazole‑amide‑piperazine‑like) intact. Modifications should be limited to the para‑phenyl ether or the terminal substituents. Avoid wholesale scaffold changes (e.g., swapping the entire phenyl for a bulky heterocycle).

  • Hydroxyl substitution on the phenyl ring: replace one fluorine with –OH (e.g., 4‑fluoro‑3‑hydroxy‑phenyl). Expected logP drop ~0.3–0.5, TPSA ↑ ~20 Å², similarity still high (>0.5).

  • Pyridine ring: swap the phenyl for a 2‑pyridyl (retain the ether). Hetero‑aryl reduces logP modestly, adds an H‑bond acceptor, and similarity stays >0.4.

  • Methyl sulfone: attach a –SO₂CH₃ group on the phenyl (high polarity, logP reduction). Must watch MW increase but still likely < 380 Da.

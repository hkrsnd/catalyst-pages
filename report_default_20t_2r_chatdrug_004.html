What do these metrics mean?
MW (Da)Molecular Weight (MW) — total mass of the molecule in Daltons. Drugs typically need MW < 500 Da for good oral absorption (Lipinski's Rule).
LogPLogP — a measure of how fat-soluble (lipophilic) a molecule is. Higher = more greasy. Ideally ≤ 5 for oral drugs. The optimization goal here is to minimize LogP.
HBDHydrogen Bond Donors (HBD) — groups like OH or NH that can donate hydrogen bonds. Fewer donors generally means better membrane permeability. Lipinski limit: ≤ 5.
HBAHydrogen Bond Acceptors (HBA) — atoms like O or N that can accept hydrogen bonds. Lipinski limit: ≤ 10.
tPSATopological Polar Surface Area (tPSA) — the surface area of polar atoms (N, O, S and attached H). Measured in Å². Low tPSA (< 140) favors oral absorption.
Rot. BondsRotatable Bonds — single bonds that allow free rotation, making the molecule flexible. Fewer (≤ 10) is better for oral bioavailability.
QEDQuantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness (QED) — a score from 0 to 1 combining multiple drug-likeness properties. Higher = more drug-like. Most approved drugs score > 0.3.
Lipinski Viol.Lipinski Violations — how many of Lipinski's 'Rule of Five' criteria the molecule fails (MW, LogP, HBD, HBA). Zero violations is ideal; ≤ 1 is acceptable.

PROPOSAL_1

SMILES: CS(=O)(=O)CCNCc1ccc(-c2ccc3ncnc(Nc4ccc(OCc5cccc(F)c5)c(OC)c4)c3c2)o1
RATIONALE:
  • Modification: The para‑chloro substituent on the central phenyl ring is replaced by a methoxy (‑OCH₃) group.
  • Why: A methoxy introduces an additional hydrogen‑bond acceptor without adding a hydrogen‑bond donor, modestly increasing polarity and reducing logP. It is synthetically accessible via O‑methylation of the corresponding phenol and is less prone to rapid Phase II conjugation than a phenolic OH.
  • Predicted effect: ↓ logP (more polar), HBD unchanged (still 2), HBA +1, MW ↑ ~14 Da (still ≈ 595 Da) – overall a small improvement in lipophilicity with minimal impact on other properties.

PROPOSAL_2

SMILES: CS(=O)(=O)CCNCc1ccc(-c2ccc3ncnc(Nc4ccc(OCc5ccccn5)c(Cl)c4)c3c2)o1
RATIONALE:
  • Modification: The fluorinated phenyl ring (c5cccc(F)c5) is swapped for a pyridine ring (c5ccccn5).
  • Why: Introducing a ring nitrogen is a classic bioisosteric change that raises polarity (adds an H‑bond acceptor) and typically reduces logP. Pyridine is readily installed via Suzuki coupling with 5‑bromo‑pyridine or similar routes.
  • Predicted effect: ↓ logP, HBA +1, MW ↑ ~2 Da (fluorine → nitrogen), HBD unchanged. The heteroaryl may also provide a modest improvement in aqueous solubility.

PROPOSAL_3

SMILES: CS(=O)(=O)CCNCc1ccc(-c2ccc3ncnc(Nc4ccc(OCOc5cccc(F)c5)c(Cl)c4)c3c2)o1
RATIONALE:
  • Modification: The benzylic ether linker OCc5… is converted to an aryl ester OCOc5….
  • Why: Adding a carbonyl oxygen creates an extra hydrogen‑bond acceptor and increases overall polarity. Esters are synthetically feasible via acylation of the phenolic oxygen with chloroacetyl chloride followed by coupling to the aryl bromide. The change is relatively conservative (maintains the aromatic ring and overall shape).
  • Predicted effect: ↓ logP, HBA +1, MW ↑ ~14 Da, HBD unchanged. The ester may also be more metabolically stable than the benzylic ether, reducing potential oxidative cleavage.
Overall expectation: Each proposal introduces a single, synthetically tractable functional‑group change that should collectively lower the calculated logP while keeping the core pharmacophore and maintaining similarity above the 0.3 threshold. Subsequent computational evaluation will confirm the magnitude of logP reduction and any impact on other ADMET metrics.
